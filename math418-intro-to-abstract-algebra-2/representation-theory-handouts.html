Representation of Finite Groups

Representation theory studies algebraic structures by representing their elements as linear transformations of vector spaces and studies modules over these linear transformations. In this presentation, we talk about how this applies to finite groups.

Linear representation of a group

Let VVV be a vector space over the field C\mathbb CC, and let GL(V)\mathrm{GL}(V)GL(V) be the group of isomorphisms of VVV to itself. Let GGG be a finite group. The linear representation of GGG in VVV is a homomorphism ρ:G→GL(V)\rho: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)ρ:G→GL(V).

If VVV is an nnn-dimensional vector space, then by fixing a basis, we get GL(V)≅GLn(F)\mathrm{GL}(V) \cong \mathrm{GL}_n(F)GL(V)≅GL​n​​(F). Since we deal with linear representations of finite degree, we can use this isomorphism to pass freely between these matrix representations and the linear representation. In other words, ρ\rhoρ associates each element of GGG with an n×nn\times nn×n invertible matrix such that ρ(st)=ρ(s)ρ(t)∀s,t∈G\rho(st) = \rho(s)\rho(t) \forall s, t \in Gρ(st)=ρ(s)ρ(t)∀s,t∈G.

Stable (invariant) subspace

Given a representation ρ:G→GL(V)\rho: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)ρ:G→GL(V), a subspace W⊂VW \subset VW⊂V is stable if ∀w∈W,g∈G,ρ(g)w∈W\forall w \in W, g \in G, \quad \rho(g) w \in W∀w∈W,g∈G,ρ(g)w∈W

e.g.: For the trivial representation, any subspace is stable.

Irreducible representation

A representation ρ:G→GL(V)\rho: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)ρ:G→GL(V) is irreducible if it's only stable subspaces are {0}\{0\}{0} and VVV itself.

An example

This means that the vector space, [xx]\begin{bmatrix} x \\ x\end{bmatrix}[​x​x​​] is not irreducible.

Since one of it's invariant subspaces is [xx]\begin{bmatrix} x \\ x\end{bmatrix}[​x​x​​], the other must be it's orthogonal complement, [x−x]\begin{bmatrix} x \\ -x\end{bmatrix}[​x​−x​​].
