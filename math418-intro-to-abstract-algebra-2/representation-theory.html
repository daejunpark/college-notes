Representation of Finite Groups

Representation theory studies algebraic structures by representing their elements as linear transformations of vector spaces and studies modules over these linear transformations. In this presentation, we talk about how this applies to finite groups.

Linear representation of a group

Let VVV be a vector space over the field C\mathbb CC, and let GL(V)\mathrm{GL}(V)GL(V) be the group of isomorphisms of VVV to itself. Let GGG be a finite group. The linear representation of GGG in VVV is a homomorphism ρ:G→GL(V)\rho: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)ρ:G→GL(V).

If VVV is an nnn-dimensional vector space, then by fixing a basis, we get GL(V)≅GLn(F)\mathrm{GL}(V) \cong \mathrm{GL}_n(F)GL(V)≅GL​n​​(F). Since we deal with linear representations of finite degree, we can use this isomorphism to pass freely between these matrix representation and the linear representation. In other words, ρ\rhoρ associates each element of GGG with an n×nn\times nn×n invertible matrix such that ρ(st)=ρ(s)ρ(t)∀s,t∈G\rho(st) = \rho(s)\rho(t) \forall s, t \in Gρ(st)=ρ(s)ρ(t)∀s,t∈G.

Example: Unit / Trivial representation

Let GGG be a finite group. Let ρ:G→GL(V)\rho: G \to \mathrm {GL}(V)ρ:G→GL(V). Let ρ(s)=identity\rho(s) = \text{identity}ρ(s)=identity for all SSS in GGG then ρ\rhoρ is a representation of GGG called the trivial or unit representation.

(Hermitian) inner product

Given a vector space VVV, a function ⟨⋅,⋅⟩:V→C\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle: V \to \mathbb C⟨⋅,⋅⟩:V→C is an inner product iff:

  1. Linear:
    • ⟨u+v,w⟩=⟨u,w⟩+⟨v,w⟩\langle u + v , w \rangle = \langle u, w \rangle + \langle v, w \rangle⟨u+v,w⟩=⟨u,w⟩+⟨v,w⟩
    • ⟨u,v+w⟩=⟨u,v⟩+⟨u,w⟩\langle u, v + w \rangle = \langle u, v \rangle + \langle u, w \rangle⟨u,v+w⟩=⟨u,v⟩+⟨u,w⟩
    • ⟨αv,w⟩=α⟨v,w⟩\langle \alpha v, w \rangle = \alpha \langle v, w \rangle⟨αv,w⟩=α⟨v,w⟩
    • ⟨v,w⟩=⟨w,v⟩‾\langle v, w\rangle = \overline{\langle w, v \rangle}⟨v,w⟩=​⟨w,v⟩​​​
    • ⟨v,αw⟩=α‾⟨v,w⟩\langle v, \alpha w \rangle = \overline\alpha \langle v, w \rangle⟨v,αw⟩=​α​​​⟨v,w⟩
  3. Positive-definite: ⟨v,v⟩≥0\langle v, v\rangle \ge 0⟨v,v⟩≥0 and ⟨v,v⟩=0⟺v=0\langle v, v \rangle = 0 \Longleftrightarrow v = 0⟨v,v⟩=0⟺v=0
Orthogonal Complement (W⊥W^{\perp}W​⊥​​)

Given a vector subspace W⊂VW \subset VW⊂V, it's orthogonal complement, W⊥={x∈V:⟨x,w⟩=0,∀w∈W}W^{\perp} = \{ x \in V: \langle x, w \rangle = 0, \forall w \in W \}W​⊥​​={x∈V:⟨x,w⟩=0,∀w∈W}.

Direct Sum

A vector space VVV is a direct sum of subspaces WWW and W′W'W​′​​ if ∀v∈V,∃\forall v \in V, \exists∀v∈V,∃ a unique w∈W,w′∈W′w \in W, w' \in W'w∈W,w​′​​∈W​′​​ such that v=w+w′v = w + w'v=w+w​′​​. We write V=W⊕W′V = W \oplus W'V=W⊕W​′​​

e.g. V=C2V = \mathbb C^2V=C​2​​, W={(z1,z2):z1=z2},W′={z1,z2:z1=−z2}W = \{(z_1, z_2) : z_1 = z_2 \}, W' = \{ z_1, z_2 : z_1 = -z_2 \}W={(z​1​​,z​2​​):z​1​​=z​2​​},W​′​​={z​1​​,z​2​​:z​1​​=−z​2​​}

e.g. Given vector spaces V,WV, WV,W, V×{0}⊕{0}×W=V×WV \times \{0\} \oplus \{0\}\times W = V \times WV×{0}⊕{0}×W=V×W

Unitary (invariant) inner product

ρ:G→GL(v)\rho : G \to \mathrm{GL} (v)ρ:G→GL(v) is unitary iff ⟨ρ(g)v,ρ(g)w⟩∀v,w∈V,g∈G\langle \rho(g) v, \rho (g) w\rangle \forall v, w \in V, g \in G⟨ρ(g)v,ρ(g)w⟩∀v,w∈V,g∈G

Stable (invariant) subspace

Given a representation ρ:G→GL(V)\rho: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)ρ:G→GL(V), a subspace W⊂VW \subset VW⊂V is stable if ∀w∈W,g∈G,ρ(g)w∈W\forall w \in W, g \in G, \quad \rho(g) w \in W∀w∈W,g∈G,ρ(g)w∈W

e.g.: For the trivial representation, any subspace is stable.

Irreducible representation

A representation ρ:G→GL(V)\rho: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)ρ:G→GL(V) is irreducible if it's only stable subspaces are {0}\{0\}{0} and VVV itself.

Lemma 2: W⊆V⟹V=W⊕W⊥W \subseteq V \Longrightarrow V = W \oplus W^{\perp}W⊆V⟹V=W⊕W​⊥​​

Proof: It'll suffice to show that ∀v∈W⊥\forall v \in W^{\perp}∀v∈W​⊥​​ ∀g∈G\forall g \in G∀g∈G ρ(g)v∈W⊥\rho(g)v\in W^{\perp}ρ(g)v∈W​⊥​​. So for any w∈Ww\in Ww∈W

Theorem: Any unitary representation ρ:G→GL(V)\rho: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)ρ:G→GL(V), is the direct sum of irreducible representations.

Proof: Assume we have a unitary representation ρ:G→GL(V)\rho: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)ρ:G→GL(V), that is an inner product that is unitary under ρ\rhoρ. To prove this, we use induction on the dimension, nnn, of VVV.

Basis: If n=1n=1n=1, then VVV is irreducible (each subspace has to have dimension k≤1k\leq 1k≤1, which just leaves the 000 subspace and VVV itself)

Induction: Suppose that the claim holds for all vector spaces WWW with dimW≤n\mathrm{dim}W\leq ndimW≤n and VVV is a vector space with dimV=n+1\mathrm{dim}V=n+1dimV=n+1. Now if VVV is irreducible, then there's nothing to prove. If we have the case where VVV is reducible, then there exists an stable subspace W⊂VW\subset VW⊂V such that 0<dimW<n+10<\mathrm{dim}W<n+10<dimW<n+1. By the above Lemma, we know that V=W⊕W⊥V=W\oplus W^{\perp}V=W⊕W​⊥​​. By inductive hypothesis, we know that WWW and W⊥W^{\perp}W​⊥​​ are both direct sums of irreducible representations, so VVV must ultimately be a direct sum of irreducible representations. i.e. since W=A1⊕⋯⊕ArW=A_1\oplus\cdots\oplus A_rW=A​1​​⊕⋯⊕A​r​​ and W⊥=B1⊕⋯⊕BsW^{\perp}=B_1\oplus\cdots\oplus B_sW​⊥​​=B​1​​⊕⋯⊕B​s​​, V=A1⊕⋯⊕Ar⊕B1⊕⋯⊕BsV=A_1\oplus\cdots\oplus A_r \oplus B_1\oplus\cdots\oplus B_sV=A​1​​⊕⋯⊕A​r​​⊕B​1​​⊕⋯⊕B​s​​.

Corollary: The representation of a finite group is the direct sum of irreducible representations.

An example

Consider the representation ϕ:Z/2Z→C2\phi: \mathbb Z / 2 \mathbb Z \to \mathbb C^2ϕ:Z/2Z→C​2​​, where 0↦[1001]0 \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{bmatrix}0↦[​1​0​​​0​1​​], 1↦[0110]1 \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{bmatrix}1↦[​0​1​​​1​0​​]. 000 acts on any vector by leaving it unchanged. 111 acts on a vector by switching it's coordinates. i.e. 1⋅[xy]=[0110][xy]=[yx]1 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} y \\ x \end{bmatrix}1⋅[​x​y​​]=[​0​1​​​1​0​​][​x​y​​]=[​y​x​​]. This means that the vector space, [xx]\begin{bmatrix} x \\ x\end{bmatrix}[​x​x​​] is not irreducible.

Since one of it's invariant subspaces is [xx]\begin{bmatrix} x \\ x\end{bmatrix}[​x​x​​], the other must be it's orthogonal complement, [x−x]\begin{bmatrix} x \\ -x\end{bmatrix}[​x​−x​​].

We can find two sub representations:

  1. On the invariant subspace x=yx = yx=y:
    α:0↦12[1111]\alpha: 0 \mapsto \frac 1 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1\end{bmatrix}α:0↦​2​​1​​[​1​1​​​1​1​​]
    α:1↦12[1111]\alpha: 1 \mapsto \frac 1 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1\end{bmatrix}α:1↦​2​​1​​[​1​1​​​1​1​​]
  2. On the invariant subspace x=−yx = -yx=−y:
    β:0↦12[1−1−11]\beta: 0 \mapsto \frac 1 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1\end{bmatrix}β:0↦​2​​1​​[​1​−1​​​−1​1​​]
    β:1↦12[−111−1]\beta: 1 \mapsto \frac 1 2\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix}β:1↦​2​​1​​[​−1​1​​​1​−1​​]

Now, you may say that those matrices aren't invertible, but when restricted to each invariant subspace they are! Taking their sum, we get the original representation:

α+β(0)=12[1111]+12[1−1−11]=[1001]α+β(1)=12[1111]+12[−111−1]=[0110]\begin{aligned} \alpha + \beta(0) = \frac 1 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1\end{bmatrix} + \frac 1 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{bmatrix} \\ \alpha + \beta(1) = \frac 1 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1\end{bmatrix} + \frac 1 2\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{bmatrix} \end{aligned}​α+β(0)=​2​​1​​[​1​1​​​1​1​​]+​2​​1​​[​1​−1​​​−1​1​​]=[​1​0​​​0​1​​]​α+β(1)=​2​​1​​[​1​1​​​1​1​​]+​2​​1​​[​−1​1​​​1​−1​​]=[​0​1​​​1​0​​]​​
